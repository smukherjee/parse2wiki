# RFP Section Taxonomy

A reference for common RFP section types, what evaluators look for in each, and what evidence typically maps to each section. This taxonomy is generic — adapt to the specific RFP's structure.

---

## Organizational Overview / Corporate Experience

**What evaluators want:** Proof you are a real, stable, capable organization — not a shell company or a startup that will fold mid-contract.

**Typical requirements:**
- Years in business, legal entity type, organizational structure
- Relevant past contracts (number, value, scope)
- Financial stability indicators (revenue, bonding capacity, insurance)
- Geographic presence, office locations
- Subcontractor disclosure and management approach

**Evidence that works:**
- Named contracts with agencies or clients in the same sector
- Quantified scale: number of similar engagements, total contract value managed, team size
- Awards, industry rankings, or recognitions (if substantive, not self-awarded)
- Audited financial statements or Dun & Bradstreet rating

**Evidence that does not work:**
- "We are a leading provider of..." (assertion, not evidence)
- Logos of client companies without context (name-dropping, not experience)
- Revenue figures without relevance to contract scope

**Common evaluation failures:**
- Describing the company generically instead of mapping experience to this specific contract
- Burying relevant experience in a chronological history rather than leading with it
- Listing subcontractors without explaining why they are needed or how they are managed

## Technical Approach / Solution Architecture

**What evaluators want:** Proof you understand their technical problem and have a credible plan to solve it. Specificity is the primary differentiator — every vendor claims to have an approach, but evaluated responses show the approach in detail.

**Typical requirements:**
- System architecture or solution design
- Technology stack and platform choices
- Integration approach with existing systems
- Data migration or conversion strategy
- Development methodology and lifecycle
- Testing and quality assurance approach
- Scalability and performance approach

**Evidence that works:**
- Architecture diagrams specific to the client's environment (not generic reference architectures)
- Named technologies with rationale for selection — especially if they match the client's existing stack
- Specific integration patterns for systems the client names in the RFP
- Performance benchmarks from similar deployments
- Testing strategies with coverage metrics from past projects

**Evidence that does not work:**
- Generic methodology descriptions (Agile, DevOps) without application to this engagement
- Technology lists without rationale ("we use React, Node.js, PostgreSQL" — why?)
- Reference architectures not adapted to the client's stated constraints

**Common evaluation failures:**
- Writing about your solution in general terms rather than for this client's specific environment
- Describing methodology as if the evaluator has never heard of Agile — they have
- Ignoring integration complexity or treating it as trivial
- Promising scalability without evidence from comparable load

## Security & Compliance

**What evaluators want:** Proof that you take security seriously and can meet their specific compliance requirements. In regulated industries, this section can be pass/fail.

**Typical requirements:**
- Compliance frameworks (FedRAMP, SOC 2, HIPAA, PCI DSS, CMMC, StateRAMP)
- Security certifications held (ISO 27001, etc.)
- Data handling and privacy approach
- Incident response capabilities
- Background check and clearance requirements
- Penetration testing and vulnerability management
- Business continuity and disaster recovery

**Evidence that works:**
- Current authorization status for named compliance frameworks (authorized, in-process, planned)
- Audit report summaries (SOC 2 Type II report dates, scope)
- Named security certifications with expiration dates
- Incident response metrics from past engagements (MTTR, detection rates)
- Specific data handling procedures mapped to the client's data classification

**Evidence that does not work:**
- "We follow industry best practices for security" (meaningless)
- Planned certifications without timeline or evidence of progress
- Security policies described in abstract terms without operational evidence
- Claiming compliance without naming the specific framework version and scope

**Common evaluation failures:**
- Claiming FedRAMP compliance when you have FedRAMP readiness (these are legally distinct)
- Describing security practices generically when the RFP names specific frameworks
- Treating compliance as a checkbox rather than showing operational evidence
- Ignoring supply chain security when using third-party components

## Team & Staffing

**What evaluators want:** Named people with relevant experience who will actually work on this contract — not generic role descriptions or bait-and-switch talent.

**Typical requirements:**
- Key personnel with named roles
- Resume or CV requirements for key positions
- Relevant experience requirements (years, domain, technology)
- Certification requirements for specific roles
- Staffing plan and ramp-up timeline
- Staff retention and replacement approach
- Subcontractor personnel disclosure

**Evidence that works:**
- Named individuals with specific project experience on similar engagements
- Certifications matching RFP requirements (PMP, CISSP, AWS Solutions Architect, etc.)
- Quantified experience: "Led 3 data migration projects totaling 50TB for federal health agencies"
- Commitment letters or letters of intent for proposed key personnel
- Staff retention metrics from current contracts

**Evidence that does not work:**
- Generic role descriptions ("Senior Developer with 10+ years experience")
- Resumes of people who will not work on this contract
- Listing certifications the company holds rather than individuals hold
- "TBD" for key personnel positions

**Common evaluation failures:**
- Proposing key personnel who are already committed to other contracts
- Not matching resume experience to the specific requirements of this RFP
- Treating the staffing section as an HR exercise rather than a competence demonstration
- Ignoring ramp-up reality — proposing full teams on day one when hiring is required

## Pricing & Commercial

**What evaluators want:** A credible, competitive price that demonstrates understanding of the work scope. In best-value evaluations, price is weighed against technical quality. In LPTA evaluations, the lowest technically acceptable price wins.

**Typical requirements:**
- Price proposal format (often a separate volume)
- Rate categories and labor classifications
- Fixed-price vs. time-and-materials structure
- Travel and other direct cost estimates
- Small business utilization targets
- Price escalation or option year pricing
- Payment terms and invoicing requirements

**Evidence that works:**
- Rate cards with labor categories matching the RFP's requirements
- Bottom-up cost estimates showing how totals were derived
- Competitive rate benchmarks (GSA schedule rates, BLS data)
- Cost-saving innovations or efficiencies with quantified impact
- Small business subcontracting plans with committed partners

**Evidence that does not work:**
- Top-down pricing without supporting detail
- Rates significantly below market without explanation (triggers suspicion)
- "To be negotiated" on any pricing element the RFP requires
- Ignoring option years or travel costs

**Common evaluation failures:**
- Pricing format does not match RFP requirements (often disqualifying)
- Math errors in pricing tables
- Not addressing all line items or CLIN structures specified
- Proposing rates that are clearly unsustainable — evaluators see through loss-leader pricing

## References & Case Studies / Past Performance

**What evaluators want:** Proof that you have done this before, successfully, for clients who would vouch for you.

**Typical requirements:**
- Number and recency of references (typically 3-5, within 3-5 years)
- Relevance criteria (similar scope, size, industry, technology)
- Contact information for reference checks
- Narrative case studies with problem/solution/results
- Contract value and performance period
- CPARS ratings (for federal contracts)

**Evidence that works:**
- Named clients with permission to contact
- Quantified results: cost savings, timeline adherence, performance improvements
- References that match the RFP's domain, not just the technology
- Case studies that mirror the client's specific problem
- Positive CPARS ratings with narrative excerpts

**Evidence that does not work:**
- Anonymous case studies ("a large financial institution")
- Case studies without quantified results
- References older than the RFP's recency requirement
- Case studies that demonstrate capability but not relevance to this engagement

**Common evaluation failures:**
- Providing references who have not been prepped and may give lukewarm feedback
- Case studies that emphasize the vendor's capabilities rather than the client's outcomes
- Not matching case study scope to the RFP's requirements
- Relying on one strong reference and padding with weak ones
